Why I Keep Making Tiny Tools for Myself

The side projects I use most are almost never the ones I would put at the top of a portfolio. They are small, ordinary, and made for exactly one person. One renames lecture files. One cleans copied reading lists. One turns a messy block of timings into something I can paste into my notes without thinking.

I like these tools because they stay honest. They are not trying to be products. They do one thing, usually because I got tired of doing it by hand two or three times in a row.

Small scope is a feature

When a project is tiny, I can keep the whole thing in my head. That means I can experiment without much ceremony and still come back later to understand what I wrote. It is a scale that invites finishing.

A tiny tool also teaches a surprisingly useful lesson about interfaces: if the audience is only me, the tradeoffs become very obvious. What is the minimum number of options I actually need? What defaults save the most time? Which parts should stay visible and which can disappear into the background?

One example I keep around

I have a small script that turns a list of tasks into a softer daily summary. It sorts the urgent items first, trims trailing spaces, and prints a clean checklist I can paste into a notebook. 

def morning_list(items):
    cleaned = [item.strip() for item in items if item.strip()]
    urgent = [item for item in cleaned if item.startswith("!")]
    regular = [item for item in cleaned if not item.startswith("!")]
    return urgent + regular

That is not life-changing software. It just removes a little friction from the beginning of a day. I think that still counts.

What they're really for

These tools are a way of practicing care. They ask me to notice what repeats, what feels heavier than it should, and what might become easier with a little structure. Some of them stay private forever. A few grow into larger projects. Most of them simply help me work with less noise.
